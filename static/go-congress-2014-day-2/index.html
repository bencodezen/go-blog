Go Congress 2014 – Day 2

By BenGoZen
In Events, Journal, Kifu
September 26, 2014
6 Min read
16 comments
G

Prelude – I – II – III – IV – V – VI – VII – Conclusion

The next day I woke up late. Since I knew that my games took a long time, I figured that it would be best for me to grab breakfast before heading off to the match. After all, the worst thing that would happen is that I would have less time. Right?
When I arrived, I found everyone sitting and playing their matches. As I looked down the pairings list, I noticed that my name was already marked with results. F? My opponent’s name circled? What the heck? Does that mean what I think it means?
As I scanned the list, I saw there were quite a few others as well. So I rushed off to find the tournament director, and sure enough, it was what I feared: forfeit.

Needless to say, I was furious. After all, the worst I expected was that my time would be reduced. I was fuming for quite some time, and tried my best to calm down. I even tried to read through the entire handbook thinking that at least if it were written somewhere I would at least acknowledge my ignorance. Nope. Nothing.
To be honest, this bothered me for quite some time. I tried my best to let it go, but I just couldn’t help myself from feeling so wronged. Some tried to tell me that it was announced during before the match, but announcements are only useful if you were there to hear it. And if you were there to hear it, I don’t really think you need to worry about a forfeit rule. And also, during the registration phase, they ask you point blank if you plan on playing in all the tournament rounds. What’s the point of that if you’re going to forfeit people after they’re late?! Especially when the rule is nowhere to be found!!
Once all the matches were over, I caught up with Michael and started a game with him. We had to adjourn midway through the game since the pro events were starting, but don’t worry, we get around to finishing the game (which I’ll show the kifu of later on).
As Michael went off to his pro event, I waited around in hopes of getting a simul game against Ho Yuu, 6p. Fortunately for me, the people who had signed up didn’t show up and I was able to get a seat.

Though I ended up losing, he told me that I played quite well and would have actually won if I had not let me connection get cut apart. It was a game I was certainly proud of and one that helped me release some of my frustration since I poured all my energy into playing the best go I could possibly play.
Everyone had dispersed at that point, so I wandered around a bit while waiting for the next simul to begin. For this next one, I was really hoping to be able to get a simul against Park Jungsang, 9p. Yes, the very same person I just had late dinner with last night! The reason I was a bit iffy on this one however, is because it was a simul that was reserved for dan players.
Nonetheless, I went ahead and asked if I could play anyhow. Thanks to Jungsang’s kindness, I was allowed to play even though I wasn’t technically registered as a dan player.

Here’s the thing. I’ve played against a fair of number of professionals at this point in simul games, but the feeling I was left with after Jungsang crushed me was incredible. Even though I was well aware of how strong professionals could be, the simul against Jungsang took it to a whole new level of respect and awe.
Once I was done with the game, it seemed like everyone had run off in different directions. Since my brother and I hadn’t eaten lunch yet, I decided that we would go ahead and go grab something to eat.
After being indecisive for a while, we finally decided on this sushi place. Funny thing is that we didn’t see anything impressive on the menu when we got there, and ended up trying their yakitori instead.

It was a fantastic time and I had a blast hanging out with my brother. During this time, I vented about my frustration with what happened and my thoughts on what I wanted to do next. To experience that injustice of getting burned by a rule that wasn’t written and available to all players really put a damper on my experience at Congress. I mean seriously, it’s the equivalent of finding out that there is a rule that your opponent gets an extra 20 points of komi if the game is longer than 250 moves.
To be honest, the rest of the day is a bit hazy to me as I try to recall it now. The forfeit was rather traumatic and affected me more than I would like to admit.What I can say for sure though, is that I spent a good part of that day evaluating whether or not I actually wanted to continue playing in a tournament.
And if memory serves me correctly, my brother and I wandered around a bit more and got Halal Guys (aka famous food truck in NY for a $6 meal that is amazing). And after that food adventure, I went to dinner with Diego, Michael, Satoru, and Diego’s friend Mark. It was a fun dinner full of great conversation and I got a glimpse into the awesome world of judo as well. While the forfeit was a big damper on the day, I’m glad I was able to end the day with friends and good times.
Update: I just wanted to clarify that I’m not in any way saying that my conduct in being late to the match is excusable or acceptable. If I could have gone back and done it again, I would have ran to Congress first and at least let my opponent know I would be back after 10-15 minutes. The reason for my frustration and rant resides in the way the rule itself is implemented / enforced. While it is common courtesy to simply be on time for things, there are numerous reasonable situations where a person could be running late. And yes, sometimes people make incorrect decisions in the moment, but it should be penalized in a way that is fair for all.

    I am going to play devil’s advocate with you on the clock issue. I understand that this was your first Congress, so you were unfamiliar with how things work when an opponent fails to show up for their game. This was my 15th Go Congress, and I had not given it much thought myself because I had never had an opponent fail to show for one of my games, nor had I failed to show for a game myself. I did however, take note of how it was handled this year during the course of the Go Congress because of the announcements, and it seemed to make a great deal of sense to me, as one who would be greatly annoyed if I had to wait until my opponent’s clock had fully run out before I could be set free from sitting idle at the board. I do understand your reaction though. You are thinking that your time is your time, and you should be allowed to have it and spend it as you please. However, I would like to offer an alternative viewpoint, which is that of the opponent unlucky enough to be waiting for you to show up for the game. There are 90 minutes for each player, so if they don’t tell your opponent to start the clock until 10 minutes after the game begins, and your clock must run out before your loss, then your opponent is doomed to sit idle and bored for an hour and 40 minutes before he can do something else. Compare that to a regular tournament game which is likely at most to be only 45 minutes of regular time, the clock is likely to be started immediately, and there is actually nothing else for anyone to do after a game is over anyway. Your opponent can’t really leave for more than a few minutes at a time because you might show up. He may not have bothered to bring anything to amuse himself with because he was expecting to play. There are other things he could be doing, such as picking up a self paired game with someone who finished his game early, or attending a pro review of finished games. Given the speed with which many finish their games he might be sitting idle after many of the games around him had been concluded. Sitting idle for thirty minutes before he was allowed to claim a victory because his opponent failed to show seems fair enough to him. It also seems fair enough to give someone a thirty minute opportunity to hustle their way to the board if they are running late. What doesn’t seem fair is that you did not know how this was gong to be done. You would like there to have been some notice of this in the printed material, but do you think you would have noticed it in the fine print if it had been there? You say you were not there to hear the announcements at the beginning of the game. True enough. But this was the second day. Were you there on the first day when players without opponents were asked to report to the TD to claim their wins? Next time when you wake up late, it might be best to show up in time to make a few moves, assure your opponent that you don’t mean to be rude, but that you must obtain something to eat to bring back to the board, and to please let your clock run while you are gone. That way you will have shown up for your game, and you will get your full 90 minutes. While your opponent may be sitting idle waiting for you he will know that you cared enough to hurry to the game, and he will be less likely to resent waiting for your return. Even if it were allowed to use the full clock time to delay one’s first appearance at the board I would contend that to do so would be a bit rude considering that setting an alarm and making a commitment to being there are easy enough to do. I’m also betting that in the future you will be there no later than twenty minutes after the games begins now that you know how things work.

    • Ben says:

      Thanks for playing devil’s advocate Terri. I was certainly hoping someone would since I realize my emotions at the time were very one sided.
      To start, I will state that I absolutely don’t think that a person should have to wait the full time before winning the game. That would be crazy. However, while I absolutely realize that it’s not fun for your opponent who is waiting for you for thirty minutes, the criticism and complaint is more-so against the way the logistics were handled. In fact, I don’t know if I made it clear in my post above, but if it were in the fine print, I would have had no issues letting it go.
      The issue with a rule like this being known to “regulars” and not to everyone is the fact that Go Congress is supposed to be a convention for all go enthusiasts, regardless of whether you are a tournament veteran or not. I can only imagine how a person would feel if Congress was his/her first major tournament and finding out a rule like this existed after the fact.
      Therefore, at an absolute bare minimum, the rules need to be written down. Just as we are told what time settings and komi are going to be set for each and every match, forfeit rules should be no exception to this as well.
      And especially with an event as large as Congress, I would also suggest that a tardy policy be instilled (especially when hosted at a place such as New York where not everyone is staying at the same hotel) where a person may call a designated number to inform the TD that they are running late (up to a reasonable amount of time). And if time constraints need to be considered, then the correct time punishment should be accounted for as well. For example, if late beyond 30 minutes, you go straight to byoyomi and forfeit the remainder of your main time.
      Finally, one of the biggest things that irked me was the nonchalant attitude that most people had towards it. “Well, it’s always been that way.” or “Guess you’ll know next time for Congress” is absolutely not acceptable for someone like me who intends on spreading go to as many as people as possible. Can you imagine how I would feel if I had brought a group of newcomers to that Congress and they all lost by forfeit because of a rule that I (who is considered entrenched in go) didn’t even know? For such a small logistical change, it’s unacceptable.
      And when all is said and done, I just want to reiterate that I really do appreciate you playing the devil’s advocate Terri. I think it’s important for both sides of an issue to be addressed (especially on a blog like mine) and I think you did a great job summarizing the opposing view to mine. Much appreciated.
