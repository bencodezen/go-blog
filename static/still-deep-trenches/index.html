Still Deep in the Trenches

By BenGoZen
In Journal
August 26, 2014
2 Min read
15 comments
S

Though I wish I was already moving on with progress on the numerous things I want to do, I’m still deep in the trenches when it comes down to it. On the upside, I will say that I have been able to really gain some clarity on what I’m planning on doing. As a result, a lot of planning (and even more planning) is going on as I prepare to take off at full speed the moment all these extraneous tasks are off my back.
And though this might sound crazy to some, I’ve got a decent influx of books coming my way as I prepare for a second look at the infrastructure of the blog.

I’ve always had a vision for how I wanted to see book reviews written and organized, so I plan to begin making it a reality as I gain the final major pillar that was missing from my collection.
On another note, I wanted to give a shoutout to my friend Nate who wrote a great post today titled Don’t Break the Problem Chain. Be sure to check it out and comment if you liked it as well!
And though I haven’t been able to get on KGS as much as I used to, I hope that everyone is doing well and winning their games. Ganbatte everyone!

        An issue I have had with Go (versus chess) over the years is
        that Go is ‘softer’ less deterministic. You mention how you ask about x, y, z and
        the answer you receive from the stronger player is not fully satisfying.
        In
        chess, usually (not always) you can drill down to a ‘final’ answer with hard analysis. Go is just
        too deep for that most times. Hard analysis is difficult because there are so
        many variations and few of them are forcing as in chess. In chess if the
        variation loses a piece – its over. In Go if the variation loses a small group
        – it may just be the beginning of things at the whole-board level. This is one reason why the Monte Carlo sampling technique works better in Go than in chess – the distribution of samples in Go is relatively smooth. In chess, most moves just lose outright so samples are unreliable.
        I do think
        chess combinations are more aesthetically satisfying (and diverse) than Go
        combinations – but perhaps that is just because I am a better chess player than
        Go player.

        • Moboy78 says:
          September 2, 2014 at 10:38 pm

          The lack of a “final answer” in go can sometimes be a bit of a good thing in my opinion. Usually the only times in go when there is a level of ambiguity to what move might be considered the ‘best move’ on the board is the opening and early middle game. It is in those two stages of the game where peoples’ individual styles of play are shown. I think people are able to have such different styles of play in go because of that ambiguity and lack of a “final answer”. Objectively speaking, move x might not be any better than move y, but it might resonate more with my style of playing go than with someone else’s. I think that makes go a more forgiving game than chess and makes those stages of the game a bit easier to master.
          I’d also like to clarify my earlier statement about sequences and the example I’d used with me asking a stronger player about a sequence. It wasn’t the variation (moves x, y, and z) that I found irksome, it’s the fact that if I don’t know that particular joseki then I’d have to spend a very inconvenient amount of time during a game reading out the different variations to see which one is best. It’s not really a big deal in casual games, but I find it very annoying during a tournament game and I encounter a joseki I don’t know.
